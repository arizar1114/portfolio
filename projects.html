A game about EDS

This was the project I worked on during my final semester, and I wrote my master's thesis about it. It explores the possibility of raising awareness for social issues, specifically chronic illnesses, though video games. For this project, my thesis partner and I chose to focus on Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (EDS), which is a group of genetic connective tissue disorders.

We started by conducting a literature review, researching relevant topics such as EDS, games to raise awareness, and games for storytelling. Afterward, we decided to gather more information by consulting with people diagnosed with EDS. To do this, we reached out to Riksförbundet Ehlers-Danlos syndrom, a national organisation for people with EDS. We sent a survey to the members, then interviewed some of them to get a better idea of what it is like to live with EDS.

After the findings from the survey and interviews were analysed, we started developing the game. We created a high-fidelity prototype of a visual novel in the game engine Ren'Py, which has its own language, but we also created some more advanced functions with Python code. In this game, the player controls the main character, who has EDS. The player goes through several days in the game, making everyday choices such as if/when to shower, eat breakfast, and more. These choices may seem insignificant, but they can strongly affect the player's energy, pain and mood. Thus, the game shows a glimpse of what life with EDS can be like.

The game prototype was put through two rounds of testing. First, we invited several people from Riksförbundet Ehlers-Danlos syndrom to test the game and give feedback, mainly on the accuracy of how EDS is portrayed in the game, but they also gave commentary on the game experience as a whole. The game was refined based on this feedback before the second round of testing, where people without EDS played and evaluated it. This time, our questions were more focused on their thoughts about the game, what they liked/disliked, and what they learned about EDS through the game. The feedback was overall positive, and our following analysis showed that there are several factors that makes a good game for raising awareness. These include the accuracy of how the central issue is represented, the quality of game assets such as sound and visuals, and the emotional factors.

I deeply enjoyed this entire project, and found the experience very valuable. My favorite part about it was working closely with the members of Riksförbundet Ehlers-Danlos syndrom. There were so many people who responded to our survey and showed enthusiasm for our project. It made me see the importance of our research. Proper representation of underrepresented groups in media is crucial for spreading awareness, encouraging learning, and invoking empathy.

The Procrasti-Not-Bot

Although much of my focus has been on digital applications and graphical interfaces, I have also developed physical prototypes.

In the course Tangible interaction, I contributed to the development of a device that we lovingly named the Procrasti-Not-Bot. As the name suggests, this cute robot's mission was to help users with procrastination problems, particularly when it comes to excessive phone use. The user would place their phone on the platform, then turn on the robot. A camera detected when a hand reached for the phone, and a warning system was activated in the robot. This system escalated with each attempt at reaching for the phone, starting with red lights and an alarm sound but eventually ending with the robot's arm coming down, slapping the hand away.

We were a group of four students. Two of us designed and built the device, while the other two (including myself) were in charge of writing the code and connecting all the electronic components, such as lights and the speaker. My proudest achievement within this project was programming the camera, which used machine learning to distinguish human hands from other objects. This was done using an ML library in JavaScript. A custom ML model was trained to recognise different people's hands and fingers, and send a signal to the robot's Arduino program to activate the alarm system. Although there were challenges during development, this was a fun and fascinating learning experience.
